# 1. Grammar & Syntax — The Code Rules

> **The Big Picture:** Grammar is the **syntax** of the English language. Just like Python throws a `SyntaxError` if you break its rules, the exam will flag any sentence that violates English grammar. These are not arbitrary "style" choices — they are logical, consistent rules you can memorize like a function library.

---

## 1.1 Subject-Verb Agreement

### 🧠 The Plain-English Concept

Every sentence has two core components:
- **Subject** — the "device" (thing or person doing the action)
- **Verb** — the "process" (what the device does)

The rule is simple: **Subject and verb must match in number.**
- One device → one process (singular verb)
- Many devices → many processes (plural verb)

**The IT Analogy:** Think of Subject-Verb Agreement like a **data type check**. A `String` variable can't suddenly receive an `Integer` function. A singular subject can't run a plural verb. It's a **type mismatch error.**

```
SINGULAR SUBJECT  →  runs, is, has, does, was
PLURAL SUBJECT    →  run, are, have, do, were
```

---

### 📐 The Core Rules

**Rule 1: The Basic Match**
> One subject = singular verb. More than one = plural verb.

**Rule 2: Intervening Phrases (The Classic Trap)**
> Extra info wedged between the subject and verb **does NOT change the verb.**
> Phrases like `along with`, `as well as`, `together with`, `in addition to`, `including`, `except`, `rather than` are **NOT conjunctions**. They don't add to the subject. They are decorative metadata — like comments in code. Ignore them when finding the verb's match.

**Rule 3: Indefinite Pronouns**

| **Always Singular** | **Always Plural** | **Depends on Context** |
|---|---|---|
| everyone, everyone, someone, anyone, no one | both, few, many, several | some, any, none, all, most |
| everybody, somebody, anybody, nobody | | |
| everything, something, anything, nothing | | |
| each, either, neither, one | | |

**Rule 4: Compound Subjects**
- `X and Y` → **PLURAL** (two subjects joined = plural)
- `Either X or Y` → verb matches the **nearer subject** (Y)
- `Neither X nor Y` → verb matches the **nearer subject** (Y)

**Rule 5: Collective Nouns**
> Words like `team`, `class`, `committee`, `group`, `staff`, `jury` are usually **singular** (the group acts as one unit).
> `The committee **has** reached a decision.` ✅

**Rule 6: Inverted Sentences**
> When the sentence is flipped (verb comes before the subject), find the subject first.
> `There **are** three reasons why...` → Subject is `reasons` (plural).
> `There **is** one reason why...` → Subject is `reason` (singular).

---

### 🔬 How It Works: Step-by-Step

1. **Find the subject.** Ask: "Who or what is doing the action?"
2. **Mentally delete any intervening phrase.** Cross out everything between commas if it's just extra info.
3. **Check: is the subject singular or plural?**
4. **Match the verb accordingly.**

---

### 💡 Simple Example

> **The sentence:** "The quality of the research papers _____ been questioned."

- Step 1: Find the subject → `quality`
- Step 2: Delete the intervening phrase → ~~`of the research papers`~~
- Step 3: `quality` is singular
- Step 4: Use singular verb → **has**

✅ **CORRECT:** "The quality of the research papers **has** been questioned."
❌ **INCORRECT:** "The quality of the research papers **have** been questioned."

---

### 🪤 The Exam Trap

**TRAP:** Test-makers insert a long phrase between the subject and verb to confuse you. They want you to accidentally agree the verb with the noun closest to it, not the actual subject.

> `The senator, along with several representatives, **is** traveling to Manila.`

The trap: `representatives` is plural, so you want to write `are`. But the subject is `senator` (singular). The phrase `along with several representatives` is just extra info. **Ignore it.**

**The Fix:** Draw a mental line through the intervening phrase. Read the sentence as `The senator ___ traveling.` Now the correct verb is obvious.

## 1.2 Tense Consistency

### 🧠 The Plain-English Concept

Every sentence — and every paragraph — operates on a **timeline**. Tense is your **timestamp**. If you're describing something in the past, every verb should be in the past. Jumping between timelines without reason is like a log file with timestamps randomly jumping from 2020 to 2024 and back to 2021. It's a system error.

**The IT Analogy:** Verb tenses are like **version control branches** in Git. You stay on one branch (one timeline) unless there's a clear reason to `merge` or `checkout` another. Randomly switching tenses = uncommitted changes across multiple branches. It breaks the build.

---

### 📐 The Core Rules

**The Six Most Common Tenses (Quick Reference):**

| **Tense** | **Formula** | **Example** |
|---|---|---|
| Simple Past | `verb + -ed` | He **studied**. |
| Simple Present | `base verb` | He **studies**. |
| Simple Future | `will + base verb` | He **will study**. |
| Past Perfect | `had + past participle` | He **had studied** before the test. |
| Present Perfect | `has/have + past participle` | He **has studied** all week. |
| Present Continuous | `is/are + verb-ing` | He **is studying** now. |

**Rule 1: Stay in Your Tense**
> If a sentence or paragraph starts in one tense, don't randomly switch.

**Rule 2: Legitimate Tense Shifts**
> You CAN shift tenses when:
> - You're comparing two events that happened at different times
> - You're stating a general/permanent truth (`Water boils at 100°C` is always present tense, even in a past-tense story)
> - You use a "time signal" word that forces a different tense (`before`, `after`, `by the time`, `since`)

**Rule 3: Past Perfect for "Earlier Past"**
> If two things both happened in the past, and one happened BEFORE the other, the earlier event uses **Past Perfect** (`had + past participle`).
> `She **had finished** her thesis before the deadline **arrived**.`

---

### 🔬 How It Works: Step-by-Step

1. **Identify the main tense of the passage.** What tense is the first verb?
2. **Scan for all other verbs in the sentence/paragraph.**
3. **Flag any verb that switches tenses without a logical reason or time-signal word.**
4. **Correct the verb to match the main tense.**

---

### 💡 Simple Example

✅ **CORRECT:** "She entered the lab, ran the experiment, and recorded her results."
*(All verbs are simple past: entered, ran, recorded — consistent timeline)*

❌ **INCORRECT:** "She entered the lab, ran the experiment, and **records** her results."
*(Breaks the timeline by randomly jumping to present tense)*

---

### 🪤 The Exam Trap

**TRAP 1: The Sneaky Switch** — The wrong verb is buried mid-sentence, surrounded by correct verbs. You skim and miss it.

**TRAP 2: General Truths in a Past Story** — Exam passages describe a past event but include a scientific fact. The fact should stay in **present tense**.
> `Darwin observed that animals **adapt** to their environment.`
*(The observation happened in the past → `observed`. But adaptation is a permanent scientific truth → `adapt`, NOT `adapted`.)*

## 1.3 Misplaced & Dangling Modifiers

### 🧠 The Plain-English Concept

A **modifier** is just a **description tag** — a word, phrase, or clause that adds information about something else in the sentence.

The Golden Rule: **A modifier must be placed as close as possible to the thing it's describing.** If the modifier is too far away (misplaced) or has nothing to describe (dangling), the sentence becomes logically broken — like an HTML tag that was never closed, attaching itself to the wrong element.

**The IT Analogy:** Modifiers are like **HTML attribute tags** (`class="hero"`, `id="main"`). They must be attached to the correct element. A `<div>` with the wrong class will render incorrectly. A description attached to the wrong noun creates a logically broken — and often hilarious — sentence.

---

### 📐 The Core Rules

**Type 1: Misplaced Modifier**
> The describing phrase is in the sentence but attached to the wrong noun.

**Type 2: Dangling Modifier**
> The describing phrase is at the start of the sentence, but the noun it logically describes is completely missing from the sentence. It's a tag with no element.

**The Fix for Both:**
> Make sure the noun being described immediately follows the comma after the opening phrase.

---

### 🔬 How It Works: Step-by-Step

1. **Find the modifier.** It's usually an introductory phrase before a comma.
2. **Ask: "What is this phrase describing? What is actually doing/experiencing this?"**
3. **Check: Does the noun that performs the modifier's action appear right after the comma?**
4. **If not — the modifier is dangling or misplaced. Fix by restructuring.**

---

### 💡 Simple Example

**Dangling Modifier:**
❌ **INCORRECT:** "**Exhausted from the long exam**, the bed was immediately collapsed onto."
*(The modifier says something was exhausted. But `the bed` can't be exhausted. The person is missing from the sentence.)*

✅ **CORRECT:** "**Exhausted from the long exam**, **she** immediately collapsed onto the bed."
*(Now the modifier correctly describes `she`.)*

**Misplaced Modifier:**
❌ **INCORRECT:** "She served pasta to the guests **that was overcooked**."
*(The clause `that was overcooked` modifies `guests` — implying the guests were overcooked.)*

✅ **CORRECT:** "She served **pasta that was overcooked** to the guests."

---

### 🪤 The Exam Trap

**TRAP:** The test gives you a sentence with an intro phrase followed by a comma. The very first noun after the comma must be the one performing the action of that phrase. Test-makers swap it with a different noun.

> `Walking down the street, **the trees** seemed to sway.`

The trap: Trees can't walk down the street. A person was walking. But the person is missing. This is a dangling modifier. The exam will ask you to identify or fix this. Always ask: **"Who is actually doing the action described in the intro phrase?"**

## 1.4 Pronoun Cases & Antecedents

### 🧠 The Plain-English Concept

A **pronoun** is a variable that stores a reference to a noun. Instead of repeating "Maria" ten times, you use "she." The noun that the pronoun refers back to is called its **antecedent** (literally: "the thing that came before").

Two things must be true for pronouns to work correctly:
1. **The antecedent must be clear** (the variable must be defined before use)
2. **The pronoun must match the antecedent in number and gender** (correct data type)

**The IT Analogy:** A pronoun is a **pointer variable**. The antecedent is the **memory address** it points to. A mismatched pronoun is a **null pointer** or **type mismatch** — the program doesn't know what object to reference.

---

### 📐 The Core Rules

**Rule 1: Pronoun-Antecedent Agreement (Number)**
> Singular noun → singular pronoun. Plural noun → plural pronoun.
> Indefinite pronouns (everyone, anyone, someone, each, either, neither) are **singular**.
> `Everyone must bring **his or her** own materials.` ✅

**Rule 2: Pronoun Cases**
> Pronouns change form (case) depending on their job in the sentence.

| **Case** | **Job** | **Pronouns** |
|---|---|---|
| **Subjective (Nominative)** | Subject of the verb | I, you, he, she, it, we, they, who |
| **Objective** | Object of verb/preposition | me, you, him, her, it, us, them, whom |
| **Possessive** | Shows ownership | my/mine, your/yours, his, her/hers, its, our/ours, their/theirs |

**Rule 3: Who vs. Whom**
> - **Who** = Subject (doing the action) → Replace with `he/she`
> - **Whom** = Object (receiving the action) → Replace with `him/her`
> Quick test: If you can substitute `he`, use `who`. If you can substitute `him`, use `whom`.

**Rule 4: Pronoun Clarity**
> Every pronoun must have **exactly one clear antecedent**. If two nouns have been mentioned, using `it` or `they` creates ambiguity.

---

### 🔬 How It Works: Step-by-Step (for Who vs. Whom)

1. Find the clause containing `who/whom`.
2. Mentally rearrange it into a simple sentence.
3. Try substituting `he` (or `she`). Does it sound right?
   - Yes → use `who`
   - No, try `him` (or `her`). Does it sound right?
   - Yes → use `whom`

---

### 💡 Simple Example

> "The researcher **who** conducted the experiment won an award."
> *(Test: He conducted the experiment. ✅ → Use `who`)*

> "The researcher **whom** the committee selected won an award."
> *(Test: The committee selected him. ✅ → Use `whom`)*

**Pronoun Agreement:**
❌ **INCORRECT:** "Every student must submit **their** project by Friday."
✅ **CORRECT:** "Every student must submit **his or her** project by Friday."
*(In formal written English, `everyone`/`every student` is singular.)*

---

### 🪤 The Exam Trap

**TRAP 1: Who vs. Whom** — The exam loves this. Use the `he/him` substitution trick every time.

**TRAP 2: Ambiguous Pronoun** — The test gives a sentence with two possible antecedents: `Maria told Ana that **she** had passed.` Who passed — Maria or Ana? This is intentionally ambiguous. The correct fix is to rewrite it: `Maria told Ana that **Ana** had passed.`

**TRAP 3: Collective Noun + Pronoun** — `The team won **its** championship.` (`Its`, not `their`, because the team acts as one unit.)

## 1.5 Parallelism

### 🧠 The Plain-English Concept

**Parallelism** means that when you list things in a sentence, every item in the list must be in the **same grammatical format**. It's the rule of consistency for lists.

Think of it like a **well-formatted array**:
```python
# GOOD (parallel) - all strings
interests = ["coding", "reading", "swimming"]

# BAD (not parallel) - mixed types
interests = ["coding", "to read", "when I swim"]
```

The same logic applies to English sentences. If you start a list with `-ing` verbs, every item must be `-ing`. If you start with `to + verb`, every item must match.

**The IT Analogy:** Parallelism is **consistent data formatting in an array**. Mixed data types in a list cause errors. Mixed grammatical formats in a sentence cause awkward, incorrect writing.

---

### 📐 The Core Rules

**Rule 1: Match the Format**
> All items in a list or pair must use the same grammatical structure.

**Rule 2: Parallel Pairs with Conjunctions**
> `X and Y`, `X but Y`, `X or Y` — both X and Y must be the same form.

**Rule 3: Parallel Comparisons**
> When comparing two things, make sure both sides have the same structure.

---

### 🔬 How It Works: Step-by-Step

1. Find the list or pair in the sentence.
2. Identify the format of the **first item** (e.g., `-ing` verb, `to + verb`, noun, adjective).
3. Check that every other item matches that format.
4. If one item doesn't match → it's the error. Fix it to match the established format.

---

### 💡 Simple Example

❌ **INCORRECT:** "She enjoys **coding**, **to read**, and **when she swims**."
*(Three different formats: gerund, infinitive, clause)*

✅ **CORRECT:** "She enjoys **coding**, **reading**, and **swimming**."
*(All gerunds — consistent format)*

❌ **INCORRECT:** "The new operating system is faster, more reliable, and **it uses less memory**."
*(Breaks the adjective pattern with a clause)*

✅ **CORRECT:** "The new operating system is **faster**, **more reliable**, and **less memory-intensive**."

---

### 🪤 The Exam Trap

**TRAP:** The non-parallel item is usually the **last item** in a list. You're scanning quickly, the first two items look fine, and you don't notice the last one breaks the pattern.

Also watch for: a list of 4 items where only the **3rd** is wrong. Read every single item.

**Common Pattern to Watch:**
> `Not only X but also Y` — both X and Y must be parallel.
> `Either X or Y` — both must be parallel.

---

## 1.6 Correlative Conjunctions

### 🧠 The Plain-English Concept

**Correlative conjunctions** are pairs of connecting words that always come as a matched set — like **key-value pairs** in a dictionary. They link two equal, parallel ideas.

**The IT Analogy:** Correlative conjunctions are like **paired tags in HTML** — `<b>` must be closed with `</b>`, `<div>` with `</div>`. You can't open one without the other. And the content between them must be logically equivalent.

---

### 📐 The Core Rules — The Pairs

| **Pair** | **Usage** |
|---|---|
| `Either...or` | One of two positive options |
| `Neither...nor` | None of two options (negative) |
| `Not only...but also` | Adding a second, often surprising point |
| `Both...and` | Combines two items equally |
| `Whether...or` | Presents two possibilities |
| `As...as` | Comparison of equality |
| `No sooner...than` | One thing happened right before another |
| `Such...that` | Describes a cause and effect |

**Rule 1: Parallelism After the Conjunction**
> Whatever grammatical form follows the first conjunction must also follow the second.
> `Either X or Y` — X and Y must match.

**Rule 2: Subject-Verb Agreement with Either/Or and Neither/Nor**
> The verb agrees with the subject **closest to it** (the one after `or`/`nor`).
> `Neither the students nor **the teacher** **was** prepared.` ✅
> `Neither the teacher nor **the students** **were** prepared.` ✅

**Rule 3: Neither/Nor is Negative — Don't Add "Not"**
> `Neither she nor I was responsible.` ✅
> `Neither she nor I was **not** responsible.` ❌ *(Double negative — wrong!)*

---

### 🔬 How It Works: Step-by-Step

1. Spot the first conjunction of the pair (e.g., `either`, `neither`, `not only`).
2. Find what grammatical form immediately follows it (noun, verb phrase, adjective, etc.).
3. Find the second conjunction of the pair (e.g., `or`, `nor`, `but also`).
4. Check that the same grammatical form follows the second conjunction.
5. If they don't match → the sentence is not parallel → fix it.

---

### 💡 Simple Example

❌ **INCORRECT:** "She is **not only** a skilled programmer **but also** writes poetry."
*(After `not only`: noun phrase `a skilled programmer`. After `but also`: verb phrase `writes poetry`. — Mismatch!)*

✅ **CORRECT:** "She is **not only** a skilled programmer **but also** a talented poet."
*(Both sides are now noun phrases.)*

❌ **INCORRECT:** "**Neither** the hardware **nor** the software were updated."
*(Subject closest to verb is `software` — singular → verb should be `was`)*

✅ **CORRECT:** "**Neither** the hardware **nor** the software **was** updated."

---

### 🪤 The Exam Trap

**TRAP 1:** Misplacing the first conjunction so that the elements are no longer parallel.
> `She **not only** cleaned the room but **also** the dishes were washed by her.` ❌
> → `She cleaned **not only** the room but **also** the dishes.` ✅

**TRAP 2:** Using `neither...or` instead of `neither...nor`. The correct pairing is **neither...nor**, always.

# 2. Vocabulary — Decoding the Unknown

> **The Big Picture:** On the exam, you will encounter words you've never seen. Don't panic. Your job isn't to have the biggest vocabulary — it's to be a **decoder**. Use the clues already embedded in the sentence and the word itself to reverse-engineer the meaning.

---

## 2.1 Context Clues

### 🧠 The Plain-English Concept

When you encounter an unknown word, the surrounding sentence is like a **debug log** — it contains clues that reveal what the unknown word means. You just need to know what type of clue to look for.

**The IT Analogy:** Unknown words are like **undocumented functions** in someone else's code. But the code around them — the inputs, the outputs, the conditions — tells you what they do even without documentation. You `infer` their behavior from context.

---

### 📐 The Four Types of Context Clues

**Type 1: Definition/Explanation Clue**
> The sentence directly defines the word. Signal phrases: `which means`, `that is`, `in other words`, `, or`, `also called`.
> `The scientist studied **osmosis**, *the process by which water moves through a membrane*.`

**Type 2: Synonym Clue**
> A familiar word with a similar meaning appears nearby.
> `The student was **tenacious**; her persistent, never-give-up attitude impressed the judges.`
> (Clue: `persistent`, `never-give-up` → `tenacious` means persistent)

**Type 3: Antonym/Contrast Clue**
> A word with the opposite meaning appears nearby. Signal words: `but`, `however`, `although`, `unlike`, `rather than`, `instead of`, `on the other hand`, `while`.
> `While her brother was **gregarious**, she was shy and withdrawn.`
> (Clue: contrast with `shy and withdrawn` → `gregarious` means outgoing)

**Type 4: Inference/General Context Clue**
> No single clue word — you must piece together the overall situation.
> `After the flood, the village was in a state of **destitution** — families had no food, no shelter, and no clean water.`
> (Clue: the description of poverty → `destitution` means extreme poverty)

---

### 🔬 How It Works: Step-by-Step

1. **Highlight the unknown word.** Read the whole sentence first.
2. **Look for signal words** (but, however, that is, also called, etc.) — they identify the clue type.
3. **Find the clue** (synonym, antonym, or definition).
4. **Substitute your guessed meaning** back into the sentence. Does it still make sense?
5. **Choose the answer closest to your derived meaning.**

---

### 💡 Simple Example

> "The CEO was known for her **frugality**; she never wasted money and always found the cheapest solutions."

- Unknown word: `frugality`
- Signal: `;` followed by an explanation (semicolons often introduce elaboration)
- Clue: `never wasted money`, `cheapest solutions`
- Derived meaning: being careful with money / thriftiness
- ✅ Answer: `thriftiness` or `carefulness with money`

---

### 🪤 The Exam Trap

**TRAP 1: The Misleading Prefix** — A word might look familiar but mean something different. `Pernicious` looks like it might be related to "pierce" but means "very harmful." Always use full context, not just the root.

**TRAP 2: The Positive/Negative Flip** — You know the *general topic* of the word but guess wrong on whether it's positive or negative. Context clues (especially antonym clues) will tell you the polarity. Don't skip this check.

---

## 2.2 Root Word Analysis

### 🧠 The Plain-English Concept

Almost all complex English vocabulary (especially in science and academics) is built from **Latin and Greek components**: a root (the core meaning), prefixes (modifiers prepended at the start), and suffixes (modifiers appended at the end).

**The IT Analogy:** Root words are like **modular functions or packages**. A root like `bio` = "life" is a library. Prefixes and suffixes are parameters or return types. `bio` + `logy` = `biology` (the study of life). `anti` + `bio` + `tic` = `antibiotic` (against life/bacteria). You can decode the meaning by reading the components, just like reading a class library.

| `-graph` | write, draw | autograph, paragraph |
| `-scope` | instrument for seeing | telescope, microscope |
| `-ology` | the study of | technology, geology |

#### 🔩 Common Suffixes (Word Endings that Change Meaning)

| **Suffix** | **What it does** | **Example** |
|---|---|---|
| `-ify` | to make | simplify, amplify, electrify |
| `-ize` / `-ise` | to make, to do | optimize, maximize |
| `-tion` / `-sion` | the act/result of | function, compression |
| `-ment` | state or result of | development, assessment |
| `-ness` | state of being | correctness, darkness |
| `-ful` | full of | powerful, meaningful |
| `-less` | without | wireless, limitless |
| `-able` / `-ible` | capable of being | scalable, flexible |
| `-ous` / `-ious` | having the quality of | dangerous, ambitious |
| `-ic` | relating to | systematic, algorithmic |
| `-ist` | person who does/believes | programmer, scientist |
| `-ism` | belief, practice, system | mechanism, capitalism |
| `-al` | relating to | digital, logical |
| `-ary` | relating to, place for | primary, secondary, library |
| `-ent` / `-ant` | one who, causing | student, assistant |

---

### 🔬 How It Works: Step-by-Step

1. See an unknown word on the exam.
2. Break it into components: **prefix + root + suffix**.
3. Translate each part from the tables above.
4. Combine the meanings for a working definition.

> **Example:** `Electroencephalogram`
> - `electro-` = electricity
> - `encephalo-` = brain
> - `-gram` = written record
> - Combined: **A written record of brain electrical activity** (EEG)

---

### 🪤 The Exam Trap

**TRAP: Prefix Deception** — The prefix `in-` does NOT always mean "not." Sometimes it means "into" or "inward." Context matters.
> `Inflammable` ≠ not flammable. `Inflammable` = CAN catch fire. (`In-` here means "into" as in "into flame")
> Always cross-reference with context, don't just rely on the prefix meaning.

# 3. Reading Comprehension — Data Extraction

> **The Big Picture:** A reading comprehension passage is a **data packet**. Your job is not to understand every single byte — it's to efficiently **extract the specific data the question requests**. You are a query engine running `SELECT` statements on a text database.

---

## 3.1 Skimming & Scanning Strategies

### 🧠 The Plain-English Concept

There are two fundamental reading modes for exams:

| **Mode** | **What it is** | **When to use it** |
|---|---|---|
| **Skimming** | Reading quickly for the big picture — the `schema` of the passage | First read-through; "What is this passage about?" |
| **Scanning** | Hunting for a specific piece of data — like a `Ctrl+F` search | When you know what specific fact/word the question asks for |

**The IT Analogy:**
- **Skimming** = Reading the **README.md** of a project repository. You get the overview without reading every line of code.
- **Scanning** = Using **`grep`** to search for a specific keyword in a file. You ignore everything except the target pattern.

---

### 📐 The SQ3R Protocol (Your Reading Algorithm)

This is a battle-tested strategy for efficient reading comprehension:

**Step 1: SURVEY (5 seconds per passage)**
> Skim the title, first sentence of each paragraph, and the last paragraph. Build a mental `index` of what's covered.

**Step 2: QUESTION (Before Reading)**
> Before reading carefully, read the questions. Now you know exactly what data you're looking for. This transforms you from a passive reader to an **active data extractor**.

**Step 3: READ (Actively)**
> Read the passage, but now you're scanning for answers to the questions you already know. Underline key names, dates, comparisons, and conclusion words.

**Step 4: RECITE (Quick mental summary)**
> After each paragraph, ask: "What was the main point of this paragraph in one line?"

**Step 5: REVIEW (Answer verification)**
> When answering, go back to the relevant passage section. Don't rely on memory. Verify with the text.

---

### 📐 Power Moves for Scientific/Technical Passages

Scientific passages on the DOST exam will be dense. Here's your survival kit:

**Move 1: Ignore Details on First Pass**
> Don't get lost in specific numbers, chemical names, or technical terminology during your first skim. They're just **variables**. Your first job is to understand the *structure* (hypothesis → method → result → conclusion).

**Move 2: Follow the Argument's Logic**
> Most scientific passages follow a predictable structure:
> `Problem → Background → Method → Finding → Conclusion/Implication`
> Knowing this structure means you know where to look for each type of answer.

**Move 3: Pay Attention to Contrast and Qualification Words**
> These words signal the most important information:

| **Signal Type** | **Signal Words** |
|---|---|
| **Contrast (most important!)** | but, however, although, yet, despite, while, on the contrary, in contrast |
| **Conclusion** | therefore, thus, hence, consequently, as a result, it follows that |
| **Addition** | furthermore, moreover, in addition, also |
| **Cause** | because, since, due to, owing to |
| **Effect** | so, therefore, as a result, leading to |
| **Example** | for instance, for example, such as, to illustrate |
| **Emphasis** | indeed, in fact, most importantly, significantly |

---

## 3.2 Spotting Exam Traps

### 🪤 Trap #1: The "Mostly True" Trap

**What it is:** An answer choice is almost entirely correct — except for one small detail that makes it factually wrong or goes slightly beyond what the passage says.

**How to spot it:** After choosing what seems like the right answer, go back and verify every single claim in that answer choice against the passage. If even one word contradicts the passage, it's wrong.

> **Example:**
> Passage says: "The experiment was *partially* successful."
> Trap answer: "The experiment was **fully** successful." ← WRONG (one word flip)

**The Fix:** Be a **literal reader**. Don't soften or enhance what the passage actually says.

---

### 🪤 Trap #2: The "Outside Knowledge" Trap

**What it is:** The question asks about the passage, but one answer choice is something you *know to be true in real life* — except it's not stated in the passage.

**The Rule:** In reading comprehension, **the passage is the only truth.** Your prior knowledge is irrelevant. If the passage doesn't say it, you cannot select it as an answer — even if you know from your IT or science background that it's true.

> **Example:**
> Passage describes an early computer that "used vacuum tubes for processing."
> Trap answer: "The computer was slower than modern computers because vacuum tubes generate more heat." ← Even if scientifically true, if the passage doesn't mention this comparison, it's OUT.

**The Fix:** Ask yourself: "Did the passage actually say this, or do I just know this from elsewhere?" If the latter → eliminate it.

---

### 🪤 Trap #3: The "Extreme Word" Trap

**What it is:** Answer choices that use absolute, extreme words are almost always wrong. Real-world academic writing rarely makes absolute claims.

**Extreme words to be suspicious of:**

| **Extreme Words (Usually Wrong)** | **Qualified Words (Usually Correct)** |
|---|---|
| always, never | often, rarely, seldom |
| all, none | most, few, some |
| every, no | many, several |
| completely, totally | largely, partially |
| impossible, certain | unlikely, probable |
| only, must | usually, typically |
| the most, the best | among the most, one of |

> **Example:**
> Passage: "Regular exercise **can** reduce the risk of heart disease."
> Trap answer: "Exercise **always** prevents heart disease." ← WRONG (extreme claim not supported)
> Correct answer: "Exercise **may** lower the likelihood of heart disease." ← RIGHT (qualified)

**The Fix:** When you see an extreme word in an answer choice, immediately go back to the passage. Did it use the same absolute language? If not, that answer is likely wrong.

---

### 🪤 Trap #4: The "Opposite Meaning" Trap

**What it is:** The answer restates the passage but **reverses the cause and effect**, or **inverts who did what**.

> Passage: "Company A acquired Company B."
> Trap answer: "Company B acquired Company A." ← Factually reversed.

**The Fix:** When verifying your answer, double-check the **direction** of any relationship described (who did the action, what caused what).

---

# 4. Linguistic Ability — Logical Flow

> **The Big Picture:** This section tests whether you can identify the **logical architecture** of a paragraph. Think of it like being given scrambled lines of code — your job is to rearrange them so the program runs correctly, in the right logical order.

---

## 4.1 Sentence Arrangement & Paragraph Sequencing

### 🧠 The Plain-English Concept

In sentence arrangement questions, you're given 4–6 scrambled sentences and asked to put them in the correct logical order to form a coherent paragraph.

**The IT Analogy:** This is like reassembling a **disassembled function**. Each sentence is a line of code. The function only runs correctly if the lines are in the right order: initialize variables first, then process, then output results. You cannot call a variable before it's been defined.

---

### 📐 The Core Strategy

**Step 1: Find the TOPIC SENTENCE (The `main()` function)**
> The topic sentence introduces the main idea. It is almost always the FIRST sentence of a coherent paragraph. Characteristics:
> - It's a **broad, general statement** — it doesn't refer to anything specific that has not yet been introduced.
> - It **does NOT start with a pronoun** (like `he`, `she`, `it`, `they`, `this`) unless the antecedent has already been established.
> - It **does NOT start with a contrast word** (like `however`, `but`, `on the other hand`) because contrast words imply a previous thought exists.
> - It **does NOT contain a specific example or detail** — those come after.

**Step 2: Find the CONCLUDING SENTENCE (The `return` statement)**
> The concluding sentence wraps up the idea. Characteristics:
> - It often uses **signal words** like `therefore`, `thus`, `in conclusion`, `finally`, `ultimately`.
> - It makes a **summary or final judgment** without introducing new information.
> - It often **echoes the topic sentence's main idea**.

**Step 3: Use TRANSITION WORDS as Logical Connectors**
> Transition words are like `GOTO` labels or function calls — they explicitly tell you what comes before and after them.

| **Transition** | **What it signals** |
|---|---|
| `First`, `To begin with` | I am the FIRST in a sequence |
| `Second`, `Next`, `Then`, `After that` | I come AFTER the previous step |
| `Additionally`, `Furthermore`, `Moreover` | I am adding information to what came BEFORE |
| `However`, `But`, `Yet`, `Although` | I CONTRAST with what came BEFORE |
| `For example`, `For instance`, `Such as` | I am an EXAMPLE of what was just said |
| `Therefore`, `Thus`, `As a result`, `Hence` | I am the CONCLUSION or EFFECT of what came BEFORE |
| `Finally`, `In conclusion`, `In summary` | I am the LAST sentence |
| `This`, `These`, `Such` | I refer back to something just mentioned — place me AFTER that thing |

**Step 4: Trace the Pronoun References**
> If sentence C says "**This phenomenon** occurs because..." — sentence B must have introduced and described that phenomenon. Pronoun/reference chains tell you the mandatory order.

**Step 5: Apply the "New-to-Known" Principle**
> Ideas flow from **general → specific → conclusion**.
> First, a topic is INTRODUCED (general). Then, it is EXPLAINED or SUPPORTED (specific details/examples). Finally, it is CONCLUDED.

---

### 🔬 How It Works: Step-by-Step

1. **Read all sentences** quickly to get the general topic.
2. **Identify the topic sentence** (broad, general, no pronoun at start, no contrast word).
3. **Identify the concluding sentence** (has `therefore`, `in conclusion`, or makes a final judgment).
4. **For remaining sentences**, use transition words and pronoun references to chain them together.
5. **Verify the full sequence** by reading it from start to finish — does it flow logically?

---

### 💡 Simple Example

Scrambled sentences:
- **A:** Moreover, studies show that students who exercise regularly earn higher grades.
- **B:** Exercise has numerous proven benefits for mental and physical health.
- **C:** Therefore, incorporating regular physical activity into one's daily routine is strongly recommended.
- **D:** For instance, aerobic activity releases endorphins that reduce stress and improve mood.

**Analysis:**
- **B** is the topic sentence: broad, general, introduces the concept without prior context.
- **D** is an example: `For instance` → it elaborates on B.
- **A** is additional support: `Moreover` → adds another benefit after D.
- **C** is the conclusion: `Therefore` → the final recommendation.

✅ **Correct Order: B → D → A → C**

---

### 🪤 The Exam Trap

**TRAP 1: The Attractive First Sentence** — A sentence with a specific, interesting detail is NOT the topic sentence. It only feels like it should come first because it's vivid. But specific details are *support*, not *introduction*.

**TRAP 2: The Misread Transition** — `However` at the start of a sentence means it CAN'T be first. But test-makers make that sentence sound like a perfectly reasonable opening statement. Always check for contrast words.

**TRAP 3: The "Dangling This"** — A sentence beginning with "This shows that..." is NEVER the first sentence. `This` is a pronoun — it requires an antecedent that must have been stated already.

# 5. Bonus Module — Proactive Additions

> These are high-frequency topics on rigorous entrance exams that are often overlooked. Master these and you'll have an edge over students who only studied the obvious topics.

---

## 5.1 Active vs. Passive Voice

### 🧠 The Plain-English Concept

**Active Voice:** The subject performs the action. → Direct, clear, efficient.
**Passive Voice:** The subject receives the action. → Indirect, sometimes vague.

**The IT Analogy:**
- **Active** = A direct function call: `user.submitForm()` — the user is the agent.
- **Passive** = An event being received: `formSubmitEvent was triggered by user` — the action is front and center, the agent is secondary.

---

### 📐 The Core Rules

**Active Voice Formula:** `Subject → Verb → Object`
> `The engineer **designed** the circuit.`

**Passive Voice Formula:** `Object → was/were + past participle → by Subject`
> `The circuit **was designed** by the engineer.`

**When Passive is Acceptable:**
- When the agent (doer) is unknown: `The file **was deleted**.`
- When the agent is unimportant: `The experiment **was conducted** at 25°C.`
- In formal scientific writing (this is very common!)

**When to Prefer Active:**
- In general writing, when clarity and directness are needed.
- Exam questions often ask you to identify the ACTIVE version of a passive sentence or vice versa.

---

### 💡 Simple Example

**Passive:** "**Mistakes were made** by the development team."
**Active:** "**The development team made** mistakes."

**Active is clearer and more direct.** The exam may ask you to convert one to the other. Know the formula cold.

---

### 🪤 The Exam Trap

**TRAP:** The exam may present a passive sentence and ask which option correctly rewrites it in active voice. A common mistake is misidentifying the subject and object when converting.

**Fix:** In a passive sentence, the "by [agent]" is the real subject in active voice. `The data was analyzed **by the researcher**.` → Active: `**The researcher** analyzed the data.`

---

## 5.2 Commonly Confused Words (Homophones & Near-Pairs)

### 🧠 The Plain-English Concept

These are **words that sound alike or look alike but have completely different meanings**. They're like **variable name conflicts** — `user_data` vs `userData` vs `userdata`. One wrong choice crashes the logic.

---

### 📐 The Master List

| **Word Pair** | **How to Distinguish** |
|---|---|
| `affect` (verb) vs `effect` (noun) | **A**ffect = **A**ction (verb). **E**ffect = **E**nd result (noun). |
| `accept` vs `except` | `Accept` = to receive/agree. `Except` = excluding. |
| `principal` vs `principle` | `Principal` = the main person/thing. `Principle` = a rule/value. |
| `complement` vs `compliment` | `Complement` = completes something. `Compliment` = praise. |
| `emigrate` vs `immigrate` | `Emigrate` = Exit a country. `Immigrate` = Into a country. |
| `fewer` vs `less` | `Fewer` = countable items (fewer students). `Less` = uncountable amounts (less water). |
| `lay` vs `lie` | `Lay` = to place something (needs an object). `Lie` = to recline (no object). |
| `its` vs `it's` | `Its` = possessive. `It's` = it is / it has. |
| `whose` vs `who's` | `Whose` = possessive. `Who's` = who is. |
| `then` vs `than` | `Then` = at that time / next. `Than` = comparison. |
| `farther` vs `further` | `Farther` = physical distance. `Further` = figurative degree/extent. |
| `between` vs `among` | `Between` = exactly two things. `Among` = three or more. |
| `comprise` vs `compose` | The whole `comprises` its parts. The parts `compose` the whole. |
| `imply` vs `infer` | The speaker/writer `implies`. The reader/listener `infers`. |
| `stationary` vs `stationery` | `Stationary` = not moving. `Stationery` = writing materials (contains `er` like `paper`). |

---

### 🪤 The Exam Trap

**TRAP — `affect` vs `effect`:** This is possibly the most-tested confused pair. The trick: both can technically be used as the other part of speech in very rare cases (e.g., `effect` as a verb meaning "to bring about"), but on the exam, treat `affect` = verb and `effect` = noun, always.

> `The new algorithm **affected** the output.` ← verb ✅
> `The **effect** of the algorithm was significant.` ← noun ✅

---

## 5.3 Idiomatic Expressions & Preposition Pairs

### 🧠 The Plain-English Concept

**Idioms** are fixed phrases where the meaning is not literally the sum of the words. **Preposition pairs** are specific verb/adjective + preposition combinations that must be memorized because they don't follow any logical rule — they are simply **hardcoded** into the language.

**The IT Analogy:** These are like **reserved keywords** in a programming language. You can't rename them or swap them out. `break` means `break` in Python, not `pause` or `stop`. You just memorize the fixed term.

---

### 📐 Core Preposition Pairs

| **Expression** | **Correct Usage** |
|---|---|
| `agree with` (a person) | I agree **with** her. |
| `agree to` (a plan/proposal) | He agreed **to** the terms. |
| `agree on` (a decision/topic) | They agreed **on** a solution. |
| `differ from` (things compared) | This model differs **from** the other. |
| `differ with` (a person's opinion) | I differ **with** you on this. |
| `consist of` | The package consists **of** three modules. |
| `comply with` | You must comply **with** the regulations. |
| `capable of` | She is capable **of** solving it. |
| `responsible for` | He is responsible **for** the error. |
| `independent of` | The result is independent **of** the variable. |
| `interested in` | She is interested **in** machine learning. |
| `familiar with` | Are you familiar **with** this framework? |
| `compared to/with` | `compared to` = similarity noted. `compared with` = differences analyzed. |
| `result in` (a cause leads to an effect) | The bug **resulted in** data loss. |
| `result from` (an effect comes from a cause) | Data loss **resulted from** the bug. |

---

### 🪤 The Exam Trap

**TRAP:** The exam will swap a correct preposition with a plausible but wrong one. The sentence will still sound vaguely okay to an untrained ear.

> `She is capable **to** solving the problem.` ← WRONG (`capable of`)
> `They agreed **with** the new policy.` ← WRONG when talking about a plan (`agreed to`)

**The Fix:** Memorize the pairs above. When in doubt, ask: "Have I seen this phrase used this specific way in formal writing?"

---

## 5.4 Sentence Completion (Logical Inference)

### 🧠 The Plain-English Concept

Sentence completion questions give you a sentence with one or two blanks. You must choose the word or phrase that logically and grammatically completes the sentence.

**The IT Analogy:** This is like **auto-completing a function call** with the right parameters. The surrounding code (context) tells you exactly what data type and value the blank needs.

---

### 📐 The Strategy

**Step 1: Identify the relationship**
> What is the logical connection between the two parts of the sentence?
> - **Contrast relationship** (`but`, `however`, `although`) → The blank word should be opposite in meaning to the other key word.
> - **Cause-effect relationship** (`because`, `since`, `therefore`) → The blank word should logically follow from the cause.
> - **Similarity relationship** (`and`, `similarly`, `in the same way`) → The blank word should match the tone/meaning of the other key word.
> - **Definition relationship** (a colon `:` or `that is`) → The blank should restate or explain what comes before/after.

**Step 2: Predict your own word**
> Before looking at the options, mentally fill in the blank with a word that you think fits. Then look at the options and choose the closest match.

**Step 3: Eliminate by tone and direction**
> Is the sentence positive or negative in tone? Eliminate options with the wrong polarity.

---

### 💡 Simple Example

> "Although the experiment was ______, the researchers were ______ to publish the results."

- The word `although` signals contrast.
- If the experiment was something bad/negative → the researchers should respond positively (choosing to publish despite difficulty) → OR if the experiment was something good → researchers for some reason hesitated.
- Most likely answer pair: `inconclusive / reluctant` (experiment was negative, researchers hesitant) OR `successful / eager`.

---

### 🪤 The Exam Trap

**TRAP — The Attractive Distractor:** One option will perfectly fit the first blank but clash with the second. Always read the entire sentence with both blanks filled before choosing.

**TRAP — The Partial Match:** For two-blank questions, test-makers include options where one blank is correct but the other is wrong. You must verify BOTH.

---

## 5.5 Tone, Purpose & Author's Intent

### 🧠 The Plain-English Concept

Reading comprehension questions often ask: *"What is the author's purpose?"* or *"What is the tone of the passage?"*

**The IT Analogy:** Every function has a **purpose** (what it's designed to do) and a **mode** (how aggressively or gently it operates). A `deleteAll()` function has a different intent and tone than a `softDelete()` or `archiveRecord()`. Tone = the "attitude" of the code.

---

### 📐 Common Author Purposes

| **Purpose** | **Signal Words / Clues** |
|---|---|
| **To inform** | neutral language, facts, statistics, definitions |
| **To persuade** | loaded language, opinions stated as facts, one-sided argument, calls to action |
| **To entertain** | narrative, humor, personal stories, imagery |
| **To describe** | heavy use of adjectives and sensory language |
| **To analyze** | breaks down components, examines cause-effect, compares options |
| **To critique** | points out flaws or failures, evaluative language |

---

### 📐 Common Tone Words

**Positive Tones:**
> `admiring`, `enthusiastic`, `optimistic`, `encouraging`, `reverent`, `celebratory`

**Negative Tones:**
> `critical`, `pessimistic`, `sarcastic`, `cynical`, `bitter`, `condescending`, `indignant`

**Neutral Tones:**
> `objective`, `analytical`, `informative`, `detached`, `clinical`, `impartial`

**Mixed/Nuanced Tones:**
> `nostalgic`, `ambivalent`, `resigned`, `ironic`, `cautious`

---

### 🔬 How It Works

1. Read the passage for **word choice** (diction). Are words positive, negative, or neutral?
2. Look at what the author FOCUSES on — what's emphasized reveals purpose.
3. Look for **opinion markers**: `I believe`, `clearly`, `unfortunately`, `shockingly` → the author has an opinion.
4. Match your reading to the tone word options provided.

---

### 🪤 The Exam Trap

**TRAP 1: Confusing Tone with Topic** — A passage *about* a sad topic doesn't automatically have a *sad* tone. A scientific analysis of poverty can be written in a completely `objective` tone.

**TRAP 2: Extreme Tone Words** — Just like extreme words in facts, extreme tone words (`furious`, `ecstatic`) are usually wrong unless the passage is truly emotional. Most academic passages have measured, qualified tones like `concerned` or `cautiously optimistic`.
